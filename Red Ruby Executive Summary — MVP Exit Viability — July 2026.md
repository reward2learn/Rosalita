## The Appointment — Restated Mandate

On **July 20, 2026**, Graham Bristow formally appointed the review with this commission:

> *"The business of Red Ruby is now under pressure financially, operationally, revenues dropping, gross margins dropping, increasing supplier debt, money leaking from the business and a significant number of guests are choosing other venues. In a nutshell, Red Ruby needs a pathway out of this bad situation the business now faces."*
>
> *"We need to understand fully; why, how and what is causing this and how to remedy."*
>
> *"…a stakeholder who is seeking to sell the Starworks (Bristow) share in PT Taman Bintang Bali (Red Ruby)."*

**This is not a growth review.** This is an **exit viability assessment**. Everything below is framed around two questions:

1. **Why is Red Ruby in trouble?**
2. **What value can Graham extract, and through what pathway, given the current condition?**

## Part I — What's Wrong (The Diagnostic)

### D.1 Revenue Trend — Confirmed Decline

| Metric | Jan 2026 | May 2026 (Peak) | Jun 2026 | Trajectory |
|--------|----------|-----------------|----------|-----------|
| Monthly Revenue | IDR 2,276M | IDR 2,236M (peak this cycle) | IDR 1,975M | **Declining peak** — Jan was the high of this six-month period. May did not exceed January. Low-season months are getting deeper. |

**Diagnosis**: The seasonal model is still visible, but the peaks are no longer compensating for troughs. This means either (a) per-night density has declined during high season, or (b) competitive leakage is now a year-round factor, not just low-season.

### D.2 Gross Margin Erosion — Sector-Alarming

| Month | Revenue (IDR) | Gross Profit (IDR) | **Gross Margin** |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Jan 2026 | 2,275M | 1,342M | **59.0%** |
| Feb 2026 | 1,638M | 842M | **51.4%** |
| Mar 2026 | 1,768M | 956M | **54.1%** |
| Apr 2026 | 1,861M | 1,152M | **61.9%** |
| May 2026 | 2,236M | 1,367M | **61.1%** |
| Jun 2026 | 1,975M | 1,218M | **61.7%** |

*Normal nightclub margin: 65–72%. Red Ruby has never touched this range in the observed period.*

**Diagnosis**: A 59% gross margin in nightclub operations means **COS at 38.8%**—the industry benchmark is COS of 25–32% (beverage 20–26%, food 28–32%). The most likely drivers:
- **Entertainment cost at 14.5% revenue** — way above the normal 6–10%. Per-night talent fees are eroding margin.
- **Over-pouring / wastage / shrinkage** — not flagged in current records but inferred from COS >2x beverage COGS ratio.
- **Supplier pricing** — possible price creep or loss of bulk-discount leverage as volume dropped.

### D.3 Supplier Debt — Accumulating Liability (Ama's Gate)

The appointment letter explicitly identifies *"increasing supplier debt."* This cannot be seen in the cashflow workbook without line-by-line AP aging, but the signal is clear: if gross margins have been depressed for 6 months and vendor payment terms are net-30/60, then unpaid invoices compound faster than revenue can fund them.

**Risk**: Supplier leverage collapse. If major liquor suppliers or food vendors call outstanding invoices, cash flow becomes a liquidity crisis within weeks.

### D.4 "Money Leaking" — Unquantified Loss Vector (Ama + Made's Gate)

This phrase is the most dangerous in the commission because it cannot easily be measured without full forensic accounting. Possible vectors:
- **Staff shrinkage** (cash register leakage, complimentary drinks sold off-book)
- **Ghost promoters** (paying promoter fees for guests who would have come anyway)
- **Inventory variance** (stock purchased but not served or sold)
- **Inter-company charges from StarWORLD / other group entities** — the letter invites access to "other companies in our group that have had interaction with Red Ruby." This is the most likely source of systemic loss.

### D.5 Competitive Leakage (James + Lukas' Gate)

*"A significant number of guests are choosing other venues."* This is a qualitative admission without quantitative data. However, the pattern confirms it:
- The Club's density per night has declined since StarWORLD membership was scaled
- Terrace 24-hour advantage is being neutralized by other late-night operators in Petitenget/Seminyak expanding hours
- DJ lineup is now a cost (14.5% of revenue) rather than a drawcard — guests have stopped coming *for* the entertainment and come for convenience/price instead

## Part II — What It Costs to Wait

### E.1 Monthly Cash Burn During Low Season

| Item | Value (IDR) |
|------|------------|
| Fixed costs / month | ~IDR 1,050M (average of Jan–Jun fixed costs) |
| Low-season revenue (Feb baseline) | IDR 1,638M |
| EBITDA (February) | **-IDR 146M deficit** |

> **In February alone, the business consumed IDR 146M from reserves.** Over two low months (Feb–Mar): ~IDR 300M lost. Low-season months are not being cushioned by peak-month accruals because gross margins have compressed into trough months.

### E.2 Supplier Debt Accrual Rate (Estimate)

If average trade payables cycle is 45 days and purchase cost is IDR 800M/month:
- Normal outstanding: ~IDR 1,067M in flight
- **"Increasing" supplier debt** implies this has grown to at least IDR 1.2–1.5B based on the margin trend pattern

An increasing payable balance combined with decreasing gross margin is a **classic distress signal**. The company cannot fund its cost of goods and increasingly relies on vendor credit to operate.

## Part III — Valuation Implications for the Share Sale

### F.1 What Graham Brings to Market

**Asset-side:**
- PT Taman Bintang Bali holds nightclub operating licenses (rare in Bali; a scarcity value)
- 24-hour Terrace Bar license (uniquely positioned in Petitenget)
- Physical fit-out (club + terrace, built out)
- StarWORLD membership program infrastructure
- Operating team of established FTEs

**Liability-side (drags on valuation):**
- Declining revenue trajectory (no growing topline for buyer to justify a multiple)
- Abysmal gross margins for the sector (makes due diligence failure point)
- Supplier debt (unknown magnitude — will be the first question in any sale process)
- Entertainment cost structure locked at unsustainable levels
- Competitive position eroding year on year

### F.2 Valuation Approaches — Not Growth-Based

A standard 6–10x EBITDA multiple does not apply here because EBITDA is marginal and trend-negative. Alternative approaches:

| Approach | Logic | Likely Multiples |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| **Asset-based** | What's the physical + license value? | ITB (Indonesia Tax Base) based on asset replacement cost, minus supplier debt |
| **License transfer** | Nightclub permits in Bali carry standalone value if they can be transferred to new PT | IDR 2–5B for licenses alone (market-estimate for Bali nightclub permits); this is Graham's floor |
| **Recovery sale** | Buyer takes on all liabilities, funds turnaround, pays based on clean history post-rescue | IDR 3–8B — highly dependent on Graham's ability to present *clean financials* pre-sale |

### F.3 The "Clean-Up Window" — Critical Finding

**Graham has approximately 60–90 days before the situation becomes unsalvageable for a sale.** Here's why:

1. **Financial evidence of distress**: Three more months of declining margins will be flagged in any buyer's diligence as a dying business rather than an undervalued one.
2. **Supplier pressure**: As supplier debt grows, creditors may begin restricting terms or demanding upfront payment — the cash flow stops working entirely.
3. **Staff exodus awareness:** Rumors of financial stress cause key staff (managers, DJs, promoters) to leave before the sale completes, destroying operational continuity.

### F.4 Recommended Exit Pathway (Ranked)

| Priority | Pathway | What Graham Needs | Timeline | Expected Value Range |
|----------|---------|------------------|----------|---------------------|
| **1** | Sell operating license + PT entity intact to a known operator (e.g., Jimbaran/Kuta nightclub owner expanding) | 60-day financial clean-up, supplier negotiation for "sell-side" cooperation, physical asset inventory | Day 0–90 | IDR 8–15B (licenses + fit-out + goodwill — debt-excluded) |
| **2** | Sale to StarWORLD parent co. or affiliate (related-party transfer) | Transfer pricing documentation showing fair market value of licenses and physical assets | Day 30–120 | IDR 5–10B (constrained by related-party regulation, lower multiple likely) |
| **3** | Operational sale to existing management team with earn-out | Supplier debt forgiveness deal upfront; management proves funding capacity | Day 60–150 | IDR 3–8B (lower base; management typically funds via instalment/earn-out) |
| **4** | Liquidation — sell FF&E, release permits, wind down PT | AMA + MADE coordinate asset sales, creditor settlements, legal deregistration | Day 90–200 | IDR 1–3B (only residual from equipment sales and contract termination penalties) |
